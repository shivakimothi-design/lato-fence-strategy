Section 1 · Content & Brand Strategy

Where We Play, Who We Talk To, What Hurts

Priority regions — concentrate, don't spread

Tier 1: Uttar Pradesh, Rajasthan  ·  Tier 2: Haryana  ·  Tier 3: Bihar, Chhattisgarh, Uttarakhand.

Virality and recall need local critical mass — ₹50L spread across the whole Hindi belt buys invisibility. Region order is validated against live enquiry data in the first 90 days.

Audience segments

Premium / Aspirational: villas, farmhouses, resorts, hotels. Utility / Considered: farms, schools, plotted homes, offices.

Trade audience: building-material dealers and distributors in tier-2 towns; architects and contractors as influencers.

User problems & behaviour

The mass desire: dignified control over what the world sees of your property — not privacy alone, not status alone. The felt problem: exposure and incompleteness — built, but not finished, not fully theirs. Considered purchase, mobile-first research, family and contractor input.

Current defaults & barriers

Brick walls, MS iron grills, bamboo screening, unbranded PVC jaali — or nothing at all. The barrier: the category doesn't exist in the buyer's mind yet. Every default has a known failure mode, but nobody comparison-shops "fences."

Structure (SB7 framework — all seven parts, mapped to the page)

1 · A character — the owner who wants control of their space.
2 · Has a problem — exposure, and defaults that cost money, labour and weeks.
3 · Meets a guide — Indonet: ISO 9001:2015, three plants — proof, not paragraphs.
4 · Who gives them a plan — three steps: Choose, Order, Install.

6 · That helps them avoid failure — one struck-through line: what staying with brick, iron or bamboo keeps costing.

Why SOA2, not every visitor

SOA5/SOA4 ads deliberately carry no site link — engagement only. Only SOA3/2/1 content and Search carry a "visit site" CTA. That's a media-buying decision, not an assumption — it's what makes designing this page for a warm, trust-seeking SOA2 visitor the right call.

Minimalism rule: no 101-level explanation of what a fence is. This visitor already knows LatoFence exists — the page's only job is to remove the last doubt and get the click.

Section 4 · One-Month Social Media Calendar

Every Reel Opens on a Locked Headline

The month's real work happened one level up: five locked headlines, one per stage of awareness. Once those exist, a reel is translation, not invention — every concept below opens on its stage's locked line or dramatizes its exact essence. So the craft went where craft is scarcest. SOA5 — cold reach, by common consent the hardest brief in advertising — is delivered complete: two finished films, two deliberately different registers, all nine fields the brief asks for. At the other end, SOA1 is the easiest line in the stack and the only one that cannot responsibly exist yet: it is offer copy, and it locks the day price and guarantee terms do. Everything in between is production against a locked sentence — scheduled, not stuck.

Post 1 — Privacy, the way Indian homes have always understood it

HP
Hitendra Panchal · 3rd+
Founder & CEO, Indonet Group
2d · Edited

My mother never sat in our aangan after evening.

Not because anyone told her not to. The courtyard was the heart of our house — chai happened there, homework happened there, winter afternoons happened there.

But it had a low brick wall. And after five, the lane outside filled with people walking home.

So every evening, without a word, the whole life of that courtyard picked itself up and moved indoors.

Nobody called it a privacy problem. It was just how things were.

Thirty years later, I caught myself turning the chairs on my own lawn to face away from the gate. Same instinct. Different house.

Here's what I've come to believe: Indian homes never wanted isolation. Hawa aur roshni — air and light — are non-negotiable for us. What we wanted was control over who sees what. Our grandmothers knew this — that's exactly what the jaali in old havelis was: privacy that still breathed.

Somewhere between the haveli and the apartment, we replaced the jaali with plain walls. And called it progress.

That's the thought we kept returning to while designing LATO Fence: can a boundary protect a family's evenings without stealing its light?

What did privacy look like in the home you grew up in? I'd love to read the memories.

Post 2 — Ownership, aspiration, and the generation that won't wait

HP
Hitendra Panchal · 3rd+
Founder & CEO, Indonet Group
1w · Edited

A customer in Rajkot built his house over eleven years.

First the two rooms. Then the first floor, the year his daughter was born. The kitchen tiles came the year the business finally turned around.

Every brick had a story. Except the boundary.

For eleven years, the boundary was a line of thorny bushes and one standing promise: "agle saal pakka."

When I asked him why, his answer stayed with me: "Deewar ke liye mistri chahiye, mahina chahiye, aur mahaul chahiye." A wall needs a mason, a month, and the right mood.

His son disagreed with the whole premise. He wanted the boundary done the way his generation finishes everything — order it, get it delivered, do it yourself, done by Sunday.

That one courtyard conversation is modern India. Ownership used to mean waiting your turn — for the mason, for the season, for the loan. For this generation, ownership means doing.

It's why we now engineer for Indian sun, Indian rain — and Indian weekends. LATO Fence installs without a single outside hand, and that is not a convenience feature. It's a cultural one.

The house in Rajkot got its boundary in an afternoon. Eleven years, then three hours.

What's the one thing in your home that waited years for "agle saal"?

Appendix · The Reasoning

The Frame, the Desire, and Why the Headlines Got the Hours

One frame, not three campaigns

Awareness, distribution, D2C sales are three levers toward one objective: profit. Awareness dominates — dealers won't back an unknown brand, cold traffic won't convert without trust.

Concentrate, not spread

Virality needs local critical mass. ₹50L spread across the whole Hindi belt falls below the frequency needed to build recall in a category nobody's searching for yet.

The mass desire

Privacy and sophistication looked like competing themes. They're one desire: dignified control over what the world sees of your life — the promise already latent in the brand's own line, fences that secure spaces without closing them in.

Sophistication stays flat, specificity rises

This is the first serious voice in the category, so every headline stays plain, not clever, at every stage. What changes stage-to-stage is proof density, not boldness.

What comparable brands taught us

Asian Paints validates desire-led over feature-led positioning for this category. IKEA's ownership-effect research is why DIY gets framed as pride, not hassle. Offered as pattern-level direction, not case data.

DIY as pride, built into the funnel

SOA4 names the ache, not the fix. SOA3 introduces DIY as a feature, not a caveat. SOA2 proves it via real installer UGC. SOA1's close reframes action as reward, not a bare transaction.

Why the headlines got the hours

The stack is the heart of this submission — a deliberate allocation, not an imbalance. Everything downstream inherits from it: the website's hero is the SOA2 line doing its job on a page; the media plan's CTA rules fall directly out of stage congruence; every reel opens on a locked line or dramatizes its essence. Five sentences, argued hard enough, and the system starts writing itself — the films, the page, the calendar become translation. Argued badly, and no volume of content downstream can compensate. That is why the hours went here first — and why the two hardest films in the plan (SOA5, cold reach) could be finished with certainty, while the easiest line (SOA1, offer copy) waits only for a price.

The DIY cascade

Confirming full DIY changed the website's CTAs, justified the "Try It First" sample-panel program, and added an objection row: "Can I install this myself?"

One reader, not every visitor

SOA5/4 ads carry no site link by design — a deliberate media-buying rule, not an assumption. That's what justifies one focused page for a warm SOA2 reader.

Why two budget scenarios

The brief's ₹50L sits directly under the 4-channel DVC ask — literally read, that's Scenario A. Scenario B extends the same ₹50L to Search/LinkedIn/IndiaMART/WhatsApp. Both built out rather than guessing which was meant.

How the math works

Every rupee = Total × State % × Channel % × SOA % — verified to reconcile to exactly ₹50,00,000 by tier, state, channel, and SOA stage.

Dealer collateral, corrected

Reused SOA2 proof content distributes directly — WhatsApp Business to the dealer list, or a dealer resources link — never posted to the public consumer grid. One owned exception: a bio Highlight for organic dealer discovery.

Built to be trued
