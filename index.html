Section 1 · Content & Brand Strategy

Two People, One Desire — Where We Play and Who We Talk To

Priority Hindi-speaking regions — and why

Concentrate, don't spread. Virality needs local critical mass — word-of-mouth compounds when a region hits real frequency, and dilutes to nothing spread thin across the whole Hindi belt.

Two audiences, so two starting regions: states with visible aspirational, multi-property wealth creation first, and states with dense, ordinary joint-family household life close behind — not a single list, a dual one, funded to reach both.

Target audience segments

The Self-Made Owner — built like "Pandey": 40, worked hard, owns a farm, a villa, and a restaurant he just built. Wealth arrived faster than his sense of belonging to it.

The Ordinary Householder — built like "Shalini": a housewife in a full, joint family, whose one nightly moment of quiet is routinely interrupted.

Why two, not one: a single wealthy protagonist reaches only aspirational viewers; a single ordinary one reaches only the broadest. Two characters, two mechanisms, the widest possible net.

Written for SOA5: both characters are built for the unaware stage — where identification, not persuasion, does the work.

User problems, pain points & behaviour

The desire underneath everything: dignified control over what the world sees of your life — not privacy alone, not status alone. It shows up two ways: ownership without control (you can own three properties and still feel none of them are truly yours), and routine, resigned exposure (a private moment interrupted so often it stops registering as wrong). People don't go looking for a fence — they carry a low-grade, unnamed discomfort about their property's edges until something names it for them.

Competing products & barriers to adoption

There isn't a real competitive category here yet — this market sits at an early stage of sophistication; nobody has made a serious, well-made claim in this space before. The job isn't to out-position anyone. It's to be the first real voice a category hears.

The barrier: "needs a contractor" is the default assumption for almost everything in an Indian home — a fence you install yourself is a genuinely new behaviour to ask for, not just a new product.

Product positioning — the position to own in the prospect's mind

Positioning is not what we say about the product — it is the place the product occupies in the prospect's mind: what it does, and who it is for. For the property owner who feels watched on his own land, LatoFence must come to mean one thing: control over your own space — deciding, on your own terms, what the world sees of your life. Self-installed, so that control never waits on anyone else.

Brand tone of voice

Say it like this: "Control your space with LatoFence."
Avoid: "Our advanced polymer technology delivers unparalleled performance."

Content buckets — what the brand should say, show, repeat, and own

Say

One sentence, matched to how aware the viewer already is — never the same claim at every stage. A cold viewer hears identification. A warm one hears proof.

Show — SOA5

Two recurring characters, not stock footage — a self-made multi-property owner and an ordinary householder, each carrying the same ache in a different register.

Repeat

"Space" and "control", threaded through every stage — from "do you own the property but not the space?" to "control your space with LatoFence" — so a viewer who sees more than one asset feels continuity, not repetition.

Own

Being the first serious, honest voice in a category that has never had one — plain claims, no comparison, no invented sophistication it hasn't earned yet.

The headline stack — the core of this strategy

Our prospects sit at different stages of awareness — and each stage is separated from the next by a psychological wall. A headline, a concept, a lead — or the essence of any of these — that works for one stage will not work for another. A prospect at the problem-aware stage will not respond to a solution-aware headline: they need recognition of the problem, empathy with it. That is what pulls them in, makes them read the next line, explore the messaging, watch the creative.

Section 2 · D2C Website / E-commerce Communication

One Page, Built for One Reader — Shown Live Below

What the first fold should communicate

The hero states the character's desire directly — "Control your space" — plus the DIY promise, plus one CTA: "Call Us" — a human voice before committing.

What builds trust — and the minimalism rule

The site is built for one specific reader — someone already aware of the brand, seeking final conviction — not a stranger who's never heard of it. Trust is emphasized around proof, not introduction.

No 101-level explanation of what a fence is. The visitor already knows LatoFence exists — the page's only job is to remove the last doubt and get the click.

Product benefits and proof points

Bare, factual proof — 18 years, ISO 9001:2015, manufacturing units — shown as numbers, not paragraphs. The same "honest, not clever" rule as every headline.

Objection-handling communication

"Can I actually install this myself?" — the single biggest objection this category has never had to answer before. Addressed head-on with the three-step Plan section, not buried.

"I've never heard of this brand." — answered with the plain proof numbers, not persuasion copy.

CTA strategy & offer

"Call Us" (direct, human) + "Try It First" (transitional) — a named sample-panel program: a first-time self-install buyer needs a low-risk way to test fit and confidence before committing to a full property.

Page flow — credibility and conversion together

Hero (desire + CTA) → Stakes (plain, struck-through defaults) → Proof (bare numbers) → Plan (3 steps, install included) → Final CTA repeated once, nothing new introduced. Proof and the CTA share the same fold — trust and the ask are never more than one scroll apart. Tone: plain, honest, unadorned.

Section 3 · Distribution Strategy for 3 DVCs

Budget, Regions, Phases — and Which Film Runs Where

ChannelBudget splitRationale
Instagram / Facebook40%Organic reach can't be forced the way paid targeting can — a new handle earns reach purely on hook strength. This is where both recurring characters (self-made owner, ordinary householder) run, across every stage of awareness.
Radio25%No visual, so the headline has to carry the whole cold-open alone. Reaches the ordinary-householder audience where a screen-based platform may not.
YouTube20%Longer-form room for the same films where a viewer has slightly more patience than a Reels scroll allows.
Theatre15%The most patience of any format — captive audience, no skip button — so the headline can arrive as a punchline after a slower visual build, not an opener.

Region prioritisation: concentrated, not spread thin — the ₹50L budget funds the highest-density regions for each of the two audiences first and deepest, rather than a uniform layer across the whole Hindi belt. Virality and dealer credibility both need local critical mass to compound; spread thin, neither happens.

Phase 1 · Cold reach

Launch, both characters introduced, identification-only — highest-density regions for each audience.

Phase 2 · Problem + solution

Retarget engaged viewers with the ache, then the brand as the answer — same regions, deepened.

Phase 3 · Proof + trigger

Warm audiences only — trust content, then the offer — the only phase carrying a real CTA.

Which DVC runs where

Emotion-led films run where people scroll for identification, not research — Meta, Radio, Theatre. Longer-form, proof-led material runs where a viewer has already chosen to spend more attention — YouTube and the website. Of the three films published on @lato_fence: the manifesto film and the neighbour-comedy carry identification on Meta, Radio and Theatre; the festive family film joins in the festive window; product-proof cutdowns hold YouTube.

How the films adapt per platform

The headline moves position by format — burned-in on-screen for muted Reels viewing, spoken after a visual beat on YouTube, carrying the entire cold-open alone on Radio, arriving as a delayed punchline in Theatre.

One asset, two objectives — consumer awareness and distributor interest

The same proof content built for consumers (manufacturing discipline, install ease) is reused directly with dealers — sent via WhatsApp to a maintained dealer list, never posted to the public consumer grid — so one asset serves both objectives without diluting either.

Section 4 · One-Month Social Media Calendar

Every Reel Opens on a Locked Headline

The month's real work happened one level up: five locked headlines, one per stage of awareness. Once those exist, a reel is translation, not invention — every concept below opens on its stage's locked line or dramatizes its exact essence. So the craft went where craft is scarcest. SOA5 — cold reach, by common consent the hardest brief in advertising — is delivered complete: two finished films, two deliberately different registers, all nine fields the brief asks for. At the other end, SOA1 is the easiest line in the stack and the only one that cannot responsibly exist yet: it is offer copy, and it locks the day price and guarantee terms do. The remaining reels needed to reach the brief's 12–15 follow the same process — locked headline first, script second. Scheduled, not stuck.

Section 5 · Founder LinkedIn Ghostwriting

Two Stories, Zero Product-Push

Written as stories, not statements — a first line that stops the scroll, a real scene, a cultural truth, and the brand arriving only where it's earned.

Post 1 — Privacy, the way Indian homes have always understood it

HP
Hitendra Panchal · 3rd+
Founder & CEO, Indonet Group
2d · Edited

My mother never sat in our aangan after evening.

Not because anyone told her not to. The courtyard was the heart of our house — chai happened there, homework happened there, winter afternoons happened there.

But it had a low brick wall. And after five, the lane outside filled with people walking home.

So every evening, without a word, the whole life of that courtyard picked itself up and moved indoors.

Nobody called it a privacy problem. It was just how things were.

Thirty years later, I caught myself turning the chairs on my own lawn to face away from the gate. Same instinct. Different house.

Here's what I've come to believe: Indian homes never wanted isolation. Hawa aur roshni — air and light — are non-negotiable for us. What we wanted was control over who sees what. Our grandmothers knew this — that's exactly what the jaali in old havelis was: privacy that still breathed.

Somewhere between the haveli and the apartment, we replaced the jaali with plain walls. And called it progress.

That's the thought we kept returning to while designing LATO Fence: can a boundary protect a family's evenings without stealing its light?

What did privacy look like in the home you grew up in? I'd love to read the memories.

Post 2 — Ownership, aspiration, and the generation that won't wait

HP
Hitendra Panchal · 3rd+
Founder & CEO, Indonet Group
1w · Edited

A customer in Rajkot built his house over eleven years.

First the two rooms. Then the first floor, the year his daughter was born. The kitchen tiles came the year the business finally turned around.

Every brick had a story. Except the boundary.

For eleven years, the boundary was a line of thorny bushes and one standing promise: "agle saal pakka."

When I asked him why, his answer stayed with me: "Deewar ke liye mistri chahiye, mahina chahiye, aur mahaul chahiye." A wall needs a mason, a month, and the right mood.

His son disagreed with the whole premise. He wanted the boundary done the way his generation finishes everything — order it, get it delivered, do it yourself, done by Sunday.

That one courtyard conversation is modern India. Ownership used to mean waiting your turn — for the mason, for the season, for the loan. For this generation, ownership means doing.

It's why we now engineer for Indian sun, Indian rain — and Indian weekends. LATO Fence installs without a single outside hand, and that is not a convenience feature. It's a cultural one.

The house in Rajkot got its boundary in an afternoon. Eleven years, then three hours.

What's the one thing in your home that waited years for "agle saal"?

Appendix · The Reasoning

Why the Headlines Got the Hours — and How Each Was Derived

Why the headlines got the hours

The stack is the heart of this submission — a deliberate allocation, not an imbalance. Everything downstream inherits from it: the website's hero is the SOA2 line doing its job on a page; the media plan's CTA rules fall directly out of stage congruence; every reel opens on a locked line or dramatizes its essence. Five sentences, argued hard enough, and the system starts writing itself — the films, the page, the calendar become translation. Argued badly, and no volume of content downstream can compensate. That is why the hours went here first — and why the two hardest films in the plan (SOA5, cold reach) could be finished with certainty, while the easiest line (SOA1, offer copy) waits only for a price.
